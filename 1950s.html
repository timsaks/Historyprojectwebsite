Decade Exhibit

The 1950s

The decade is often remembered for the way that women went back to what had been perceived "standard," job-wise. They took on service-oriented positions and reassumed their positions as matriarchs of the house after the end of World War II.

Exhibits Curated by Sarah Turk

1956

Father Knows Best

First airing on October 3rd, 1954, the TV sitcom “Father Knows Best” aimed to represent what became known as “the ideal family,” featuring the father as the breadwinner and the mother as the “matriarch of the house.” As you watch a clip from this episode, “Margaret Is A 'Big Shot At School'” observe the family dynamics. The father is coming home, likely from work, and his three children embrace him while his wife sets up the table for the dinner she had likely been cooking. Regardless of the conversations going on throughout the episode, these dynamics permeate the entire franchise and set a path for the revival of this family style in the 1950s. This artifact is an accurately timed representation that encapsulates exactly what the ideas of the 1950s were. This sitcom became extremely popular and is still watched today, although a few people nowadays criticize it for the extremes it goes to with regards to gender roles. The father, Jim, is characterized as wise and is actively a manager in the working class, and the mother, Margaret, is seen as soft, sensible, and a “model housewife.” The two girls in the family, Betty and Kathy, are likely embodiments of what was expected of younger women at the time. This video shows a shift back from the force of the women’s power movement and a reversion to “old” ideals as a result of the end of the Second World War, which was when several women began to acquire more diverse jobs. These jobs were absolutely necessary at a time when there was an increase in defense and manufacturing to match the intensity needed to win the war. Women worked in aviation, shipbuilding, technology, and medicine, but that was suddenly all taken away due to the lack of demand for it after World War II was over.

1957

Stewardesses in Training

This photo was taken on an airplane, hence the outfits worn by the three women. Here, an instructor at the American Airlines training school in Chicago, Illinois, teaches two rising stewardesses how to serve food. At this school, every plane-related hospitality subject is taught, from flight routes to first aid, and girls must qualify to attend and become stewardesses. This picture was taken in 1957, at a time when “pink-collar” jobs were becoming prevalent post-World War II. While the name “pink-collar” had not actually been coined until the 1970s, certain jobs were commonly seen as exclusive to women or “women-only”. These jobs tended to be related to caregiving, which was meant to both replicate and slightly expand upon women’s roles in the home. This artifact is another example of women’s reversion to family-style or service-oriented occupations, displaying the bounce back from their defense-heavy jobs in World War II. It accurately portrays some of the new jobs women took instead upon the removal of their old wartime careers. In the 1950s, stewardesses were almost exclusively female, taking up about 99% of the profession. Strangely, female stewardesses were also supposed to be unmarried and were supposedly fired if they got married, likely because the business was attempting to use them to appeal to more men. This displays one of the many strange “rules” set up by businesses to try to reduce or “control” the number of women in certain occupations. Again, this photo displays the shift back from women’s push to acquire jobs in the workforce, instead showing the way they looked more service-oriented careers. Both this and the “Father Knows Best” show portray women in what was previously thought of as their “customary” occupations, but we start to see more diversity post-World War II in the next 20+ years. However, women’s jobs in the workforce had expanded slightly out from home-based jobs in the 1950s, which can be seen in the population of nurses, stewardesses, and teachers within the decade.
