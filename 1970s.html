Decade Exhibit

The 1970s

American women made big strides in the 1970s as they pushed for legislation that would expand their opportunities and protect their rights in the workforce. They gained financial independence and respect in their fields.

Exhibits Curated by Elizabeth Whittle

1973

Battle of the Sexes Tennis Match

This is the tennis dress that Billie Jean King wore on September 20, 1973, during the famous Battle of the Sexes match between her and Bobby Riggs. At the time, Riggs was a 55-year-old retired tennis player, and Billie Jean King was one of, if not the, top players in tennis. A year earlier, President Nixon had signed Title IX into law, which outlawed sex-based discrimination in educational and federally-funded programs. As many women tennis players were increasingly pushing for equal opportunities in their sport, Riggs repeatedly made misogynistic, attention-seeking comments about women tennis players, saying that they were inferior and that they should stop complaining. Riggs challenged King to a match earlier in 1973, believing that he could defeat any female tennis player even though he was retired. When King declined, Riggs instead challenged Margaret Court, another professional player who was taken off guard by the request. Riggs won the match, along with $100,000. King saw this and decided to take on Riggs to prove him wrong. She wore this iconic tennis dress, which Ted Tinling designed, for the match. It has an intricate tree branch design adorned with light blue rhinestones. King won decisively by beating Riggs in straight sets (6-4, 6-3, 6-3). The Battle of the Sexes match came at a pivotal time in women’s sports. King felt a massive amount of pressure to represent her entire gender and prove they deserved to be respected and taken seriously in professional sports. King has continued to expand opportunities for women in professional sports. A year after this match, in 1974, she founded the Women’s Sports Foundation, an advocacy group that funds research on women in sports, provides sports opportunities for girls, and defends key legislation like Title IX. King also supported the founding of the Professional Women’s Hockey League in 2023 as one of its board members, and its MVP award (its highest award) is named after her.

1974

Equal Credit Opportunity Act

This is an American Express credit card that expired in October 1961 and belonged to the jazz legend Ella Fitzergald. Up until 1974, women had limited opportunities to have their own access to credit. Banks would often disallow women from opening accounts, starting businesses, or making large purchases unless they had a man sign off. Married women could only own credit cards in their husbands’ names, causing credit history to belong to husbands instead of wives even if they got divorced. Women like Fitzgerald who did have their own credit cards were not guaranteed to be able to make their own financial decisions and transactions without discrimination from banks. Sometimes, banks would even require women to provide confirmation that they could not have children before granting them loans because a common assumption at the time was that women would stop working once they became pregnant. Up until the passage of the Pregnancy Discrimination Act of 1978, businesses often fired women once they became pregnant because of the false and unfair belief that they would become incapable of working. Working fathers were not subject to the same treatment. In 1974, President Ford passed the Equal Credit Opportunity Act (ECOA), which forbids creditors from unjustly denying credit access to applicants because of discrimination on any of several different bases, including on the basis of sex. The bill was drafted by Emily Card, a Congressional fellow for Senator William Brock who herself had been denied the ability to own a credit card unless her husband signed for her. ECOA made it possible for American women to take control of their financial lives and well-being without having to rely on their husbands or male relatives. Working women, although still earning substantially lower wages than men with similar jobs, now had their own financial autonomy that was protected by law. They could become entrepreneurs in their own right and take out loans in their own names to advance their careers in ways they could not before.
